Oratile Wadikonyana

Chief Executive Officer

A member of GMHPN and a 20-year-old mental health activist, certified counselor and social worker. I also wrote a book called "Taking the Mask Off," which details my battle with depression and suicide, my admission to Sbrana Psychiatric Hospital, and how I recovered and converted the battle into something positive. Community service and mental health advocacy are two things that I am enthusiastic about. I struggled with depression for almost eight years (2012-2019), half of which I didn't understand and had no one to turn to for support. After attempting suicide numerous times without anyone noticing, I was finally caught in October 2018 and admitted to Sbrana Psychiatric Hospital. The subsequent stigma, prejudice, rejections, and titles were terrible and made matters worse.But I was able to recover. After that, I made it my mission to improve someone else's healing process.I don't want anyone else to go through what I did, and I believe that raising awareness and assisting people who are affected in any way possible is the greatest way to do it. That's how BTI came to be, and I'm confident that with this incredible team, we'll be able to make a difference in people's lives. Kagiso Wadikonyana

Co Founder & Brother

I am Kagiso Wadikonyana, a second year student in University of Botswana and the brother of Oratile. I am come from a village called Molapowabojang where I attended my primary school education and Junior secondary. I attended my Senior school in Lobatse. I love nature, learning new skills and people. I am a Christian and passionate about science, health and wellbeing. I joined BTI after witnessing the dark effects of mental illnesses first hand and just how much they are misunderstood and the stigma around them. It is my hope that through BTI people will understand what mental illnesses are all about and break their stigma against the illnesses. Patients with these illnesses deserve as much love, attention and care as patients of any other physical illness.
